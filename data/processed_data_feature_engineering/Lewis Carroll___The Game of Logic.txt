<s> "Some new Cakes are nice" </s>
<s> "No new Cakes are nice" </s>
<s> "All new cakes are nice" </s>
<s> There are three 'PROPOSITIONS' for you--the only three kinds we are going to use in this Game: and the first thing to be done is to learn how to express them on the Board </s>
<s> Let us begin with "Some new Cakes are nice" </s>
<s> But before doing so, a remark has to be made--one that is rather important, and by no means easy to understand all in a moment: so please to read this VERY carefully </s>
<s> The world contains many THINGS (such as "Buns", "Babies", "Beetles" </s>
<s> "Battledores" </s>
<s> &c.); and these Things possess many ATTRIBUTES (such as "baked", "beautiful", "black", "broken", &c.: in fact, whatever can be "attributed to", that is "said to belong to", any Thing, is an Attribute) </s>
<s> Whenever we wish to mention a Thing, we use a SUBSTANTIVE: when we wish to mention an Attribute, we use an ADJECTIVE </s>
<s> People have asked the question "Can a Thing exist without any Attributes belonging to it" </s>
<s> It is a very puzzling question, and I'm not going to try to answer it: let us turn up our noses, and treat it with contemptuous silence, as if it really wasn't worth noticing </s>
<s> But, if they put it the other way, and ask "Can an Attribute exist without any Thing for it to belong to"?, we may say at once "No: no more than a Baby could go a railway-journey with no one to take care of it" </s>
<s> You never saw "beautiful" floating about in the air, or littered about on the floor, without any Thing to BE beautiful, now did you </s>
<s> And now what am I driving at, in all this long rigmarole </s>
<s> It is this </s>
<s> You may put "is" or "are" between names of two THINGS (for example, "some Pigs are fat Animals"), or between the names of two ATTRIBUTES (for example, "pink is light-red"), and in each case it will make good sense </s>
<s> But, if you put "is" or "are" between the name of a THING and the name of an ATTRIBUTE (for example, "some Pigs are pink"), you do NOT make good sense (for how can a Thing BE an Attribute?) unless you have an understanding with the person to whom you are speaking </s>
<s> And the simplest understanding would, I think, be this--that the Substantive shall be supposed to be repeated at the end of the sentence, so that the sentence, if written out in full, would be "some Pigs are pink (Pigs)" </s>
<s> And now the word "are" makes quite good sense </s>
<s> Thus, in order to make good sense of the Proposition "some new Cakes are nice", we must suppose it to be written out in full, in the form "some new Cakes are nice (Cakes)" </s>
<s> Now this contains two 'TERMS'--"new Cakes" being one of them, and "nice (Cakes)" the other </s>
<s> "New Cakes," being the one we are talking about, is called the 'SUBJECT' of the Proposition, and "nice (Cakes)" the 'PREDICATE' </s>
<s> Also this Proposition is said to be a 'PARTICULAR' one, since it does not speak of the WHOLE of its Subject, but only of a PART of it </s>
<s> The other two kinds are said to be 'UNIVERSAL', because they speak of the WHOLE of their Subjects--the one denying niceness, and the other asserting it, of the WHOLE class of "new Cakes" </s>
<s> Lastly, if you would like to have a definition of the word 'PROPOSITION' itself, you may take this:--"a sentence stating that some, or none, or all, of the Things belonging to a certain class, called its 'Subject', are also Things belonging to a certain other class, called its 'Predicate'" </s>
<s> You will find these seven words--PROPOSITION, ATTRIBUTE, TERM, SUBJECT, PREDICATE, PARTICULAR, UNIVERSAL--charmingly useful, if any friend should happen to ask if you have ever studied Logic </s>
<s> Mind you bring all seven words into your answer, and you friend will go away deeply impressed--'a sadder and a wiser man' </s>
<s> Now please to look at the smaller Diagram on the Board, and suppose it to be a cupboard, intended for all the Cakes in the world (it would have to be a good large one, of course) </s>
<s> And let us suppose all the new ones to be put into the upper half (marked 'x'), and all the rest (that is, the NOT-new ones) into the lower half (marked 'x'') </s>
<s> Thus the lower half would contain ELDERLY Cakes, AGED Cakes, ANTE-DILUVIAN Cakes--if there are any: I haven't seen many, myself--and so on </s>
<s> Let us also suppose all the nice Cakes to be put into the left-hand half (marked 'y'), and all the rest (that is, the not-nice ones) into the right-hand half (marked 'y'') </s>
<s> At present, then, we must understand x to mean "new", x' "not-new", y "nice", and y' "not-nice" </s>
<s> And now what kind of Cakes would you expect to find in compartment No </s>
<s> 5 </s>
<s> It is part of the upper half, you see; so that, if it has any Cakes in it, they must be NEW: and it is part of the left-hand half; so that they must be NICE </s>
<s> Hence if there are any Cakes in this compartment, they must have the double 'ATTRIBUTE' "new and nice": or, if we use letters, the must be "x y" </s>
<s> Observe that the letters x, y are written on two of the edges of this compartment </s>
<s> This you will find a very convenient rule for knowing what Attributes belong to the Things in any compartment </s>
<s> Take No </s>
<s> 7, for instance </s>
<s> If there are any Cakes there, they must be "x' y", that is, they must be "not-new and nice" </s>
<s> Now let us make another agreement--that a red counter in a compartment shall mean that it is 'OCCUPIED', that is, that there are SOME Cakes in it </s>
<s> (The word 'some,' in Logic, means 'one or more' so that a single Cake in a compartment would be quite enough reason for saying "there are SOME Cakes here") </s>
<s> Also let us agree that a grey counter in a compartment shall mean that it is 'EMPTY', that is that there are NO Cakes in it </s>
<s> In the following Diagrams, I shall put '1' (meaning 'one or more') where you are to put a RED counter, and '0' (meaning 'none') where you are to put a GREY one </s>
<s> As the Subject of our Proposition is to be "new Cakes", we are only concerned, at present, with the UPPER half of the cupboard, where all the Cakes have the attribute x, that is, "new" </s>
<s> Now, fixing our attention on this upper half, suppose we found it marked like this, ----------- | | | | 1 | | | | | ----------- that is, with a red counter in No </s>
<s> 5 </s>
<s> What would this tell us, with regard to the class of "new Cakes" </s>
<s> Would it not tell us that there are SOME of them in the x y-compartment </s>

<s> 5, and ask ourselves the meaning of ----------- | | | | 0 | | | | | ----------- This tells us that the x y-compartment is EMPTY, which we may express by "no x are y", or, "no new Cakes are nice" </s>

<s> ----------- | | | | 1 | 1 | | | | ----------- I hope you will not have much trouble in making out that this represents a DOUBLE Proposition: namely, "some x are y, AND some are y'," i.e </s>

<s> The following is a little harder, perhaps: ----------- | | | | 0 | 0 | | | | ----------- This means "no x are y, AND none are y'," i.e </s>
<s> "no new are nice, AND none are not-nice": which leads to the rather curious result that "no new exist," i.e </s>

<s> This is because "nice" and "not-nice" make what we call an 'EXHAUSTIVE' division of the class "new Cakes": i.e </s>

<s> And now suppose you had to represent, with counters the contradictory to "no Cakes are new", which would be "some Cakes are new", or, putting letters for words, "some Cakes are x", how would you do it </s>
<s> This will puzzle you a little, I expect </s>
<s> Evidently you must put a red counter SOMEWHERE in the x-half of the cupboard, since you know there are SOME new Cakes </s>

<s> Two more remarks about Propositions need to be made </s>
<s> One is that, in every Proposition beginning with "some" or "all", the ACTUAL EXISTENCE of the 'Subject' is asserted </s>

<s> Syllogisms Now suppose we divide our Universe of Things in three ways, with regard to three different Attributes </s>
<s> Out of these three Attributes, we may make up three different couples (for instance, if they were a, b, c, we might make up the three couples ab, ac, bc) </s>
<s> Also suppose we have two Propositions given us, containing two of these three couples, and that from them we can prove a third Proposition containing the third couple </s>
<s> (For example, if we divide our Universe for m, x, and y; and if we have the two Propositions given us, "no m are x'" and "all m' are y", containing the two couples mx and my, it might be possible to prove from them a third Proposition, containing x and y.) <s> Evidently, ONE of the Attributes must occur in both Premisses; or else one must occur in ONE Premiss, and its CONTRADICTORY in the other </s>
<s> In the first case (when, for example, the Premisses are "some m are x" and "no m are y'") the Term, which occurs twice, is called 'THE MIDDLE TERM', because it serves as a sort of link between the other two Terms </s>
<s> In the second case (when, for example, the Premisses are "no m are x'" and "all m' are y") the two Terms, which contain these contradictory Attributes, may be called 'THE MIDDLE TERMS' </s>

<s> Let me mention, in conclusion, that you may perhaps meet with logical treatises in which it is not assumed that any Thing EXISTS at all, by "some x are y" is understood to mean "the Attributes x, y are COMPATIBLE, so that a Thing can have both at once", and "no x are y" to mean "the Attributes x, y are INCOMPATIBLE, so that nothing can have both at once" </s>
<s> In such treatises, Propositions have quite different meanings from what they have in our 'Game of Logic', and it will be well to understand exactly what the difference is </s>
<s> First take "some x are y" </s>
<s> Here WE understand "are" to mean "are, as an actual FACT"--which of course implies that some x-Things EXIST </s>
<s> But THEY (the writers of these other treatises) only understand "are" to mean "CAN be", which does not at all imply that any EXIST </s>
<s> So they mean LESS than we do: our meaning includes theirs (for of course "some x ARE y" includes "some x CAN BE y"), but theirs does NOT include ours </s>
<s> For example, "some Welsh hippopotami are heavy" would be TRUE, according to these writers (since the Attributes "Welsh" and "heavy" are quite COMPATIBLE in a hippopotamus), but it would be FALSE in our Game (since there are no Welsh hippopotami to BE heavy) </s>
<s> Secondly, take "no x are y" </s>
<s> Here WE only understand "are" to mean "are, as an actual FACT"--which does not at all imply that no x CAN be y </s>
<s> But THEY understand the Proposition to mean, not only that none ARE y, but that none CAN POSSIBLY be y </s>
<s> So they mean more than we do: their meaning includes ours (for of course "no x CAN be y" includes "no x ARE y"), but ours does NOT include theirs </s>
<s> For example, "no Policemen are eight feet high" would be TRUE in our Game (since, as an actual fact, no such splendid specimens are ever found), but it would be FALSE, according to these writers (since the Attributes "belonging to the Police Force" and "eight feet high" are quite COMPATIBLE: there is nothing to PREVENT a Policeman from growing to that height, if sufficiently rubbed with Rowland's Macassar Oil--which said to make HAIR grow, when rubbed on hair, and so of course will make a POLICEMAN grow, when rubbed on a Policeman) </s>
<s> Thirdly, take "all x are y", which consists of the two partial Propositions "some x are y" and "no x are y'" </s>
<s> Here, of course, the treatises mean LESS than we do in the FIRST part, and more than we do in the SECOND </s>
<s> But the two operations don't balance each other--any more than you can console a man, for having knocked down one of his chimneys, by giving him an extra door-step </s>
<s> If you meet with Syllogisms of this kind, you may work them, quite easily, by the system I have given you: you have only to make 'are' mean 'are CAPABLE of being', and all will go smoothly </s>
<s> For "some x are y" will become "some x are capable of being y", that is, "the Attributes x, y are COMPATIBLE" </s>
<s> And "no x are y" will become "no x are capable of being y", that is, "the Attributes x, y are INCOMPATIBLE" </s>
<s> And, of course, "all x are y" will become "some x are capable of being y, and none are capable of being y'", that is, "the Attributes x, y are COMPATIBLE, and the Attributes x, y' are INCOMPATIBLE" </s>

<s> Fallacies </s>
<s> And so you think, do you, that the chief use of Logic, in real life, is to deduce Conclusions from workable Premisses, and to satisfy yourself that the Conclusions, deduced by other people, are correct </s>
<s> I only wish it were </s>
<s> Society would be much less liable to panics and other delusions, and POLITICAL life, especially, would be a totally different thing, if even a majority of the arguments, that scattered broadcast over the world, were correct </s>
<s> But it is all the other way, I fear </s>
<s> For ONE workable Pair of Premisses (I mean a Pair that lead to a logical Conclusion) that you meet with in reading your newspaper or magazine, you will probably find FIVE that lead to no Conclusion at all: and, even when the Premisses ARE workable, for ONE instance, where the writer draws a correct Conclusion, there are probably TEN where he draws an incorrect one </s>
<s> In the first case, you may say "the PREMISSES are fallacious": in the second, "the CONCLUSION is fallacious" </s>
<s> The chief use you will find, in such Logical skill as this Game may teach you, will be in detecting 'FALLACIES' of these two kinds </s>
<s> The first kind of Fallacy--'Fallacious Premisses'--you will detect when, after marking them on the larger Diagram, you try to transfer the marks to the smaller </s>
<s> You will take its four compartments, one by one, and ask, for each in turn, "What mark can I place HERE"?; and in EVERY one the answer will be "No information"!, showing that there is NO CONCLUSION AT ALL </s>
<s> For instance, "All soldiers are brave; Some Englishmen are brave </s>
<s> &there4 Some Englishmen are soldiers" </s>
<s> looks uncommonly LIKE a Syllogism, and might easily take in a less experienced Logician </s>
<s> But YOU are not to be caught by such a trick </s>
<s> You would simply set out the Premisses, and would then calmly remark "Fallacious PREMISSES"!: you wouldn't condescend to ask what CONCLUSION the writer professed to draw--knowing that, WHATEVER it is, it MUST be wrong </s>
<s> You would be just as safe as that wise mother was, who said "Mary, just go up to the nursery, and see what Baby's doing, AND TELL HIM NOT TO DO IT" </s>
<s> The other kind of Fallacy--'Fallacious Conclusion'--you will not detect till you have marked BOTH Diagrams, and have read off the correct Conclusion, and have compared it with the Conclusion which the writer has drawn </s>
<s> But mind, you mustn't say "FALLACIOUS Conclusion," simply because it is not IDENTICAL with the correct one: it may be a PART of the correct Conclusion, and so be quite correct, AS FAR AS IT GOES </s>
<s> In this case you would merely remark, with a pitying smile, "DEFECTIVE Conclusion" </s>
<s> Suppose, of example, you were to meet with this Syllogism:-- "All unselfish people are generous; No misers are generous </s>
<s> &there4 No misers are unselfish" </s>
<s> the Premisses of which might be thus expressed in letters:-- "All x' are m; No y are m" </s>
<s> Here the correct Conclusion would be "All x' are y'" (that is, "All unselfish people are not misers"), while the Conclusion, drawn by the writer, is "No y are x'," (which is the same as "No x' are y," and so is PART of "All x' are y'".) Here you would simply say "DEFECTIVE Conclusion" </s>
<s> The same thing would happen, if you were in a confectioner's shop, and if a little boy were to come in, put down twopence, and march off triumphantly with a single penny-bun </s>
<s> You would shake your head mournfully, and would remark "Defective Conclusion </s>
<s> Poor little chap" </s>
<s> And perhaps you would ask the young lady behind the counter whether she would let YOU eat the bun, which the little boy had paid for and left behind him: and perhaps SHE would reply "Sha'n't" </s>
<s> But if, in the above example, the writer had drawn the Conclusion "All misers are selfish" (that is, "All y are x"), this would be going BEYOND his legitimate rights (since it would assert the EXISTENCE of y, which is not contained in the Premisses), and you would very properly say "Fallacious Conclusion" </s>
<s> Now, when you read other treatises on Logic, you will meet with various kinds of (so-called) 'Fallacies' which are by no means ALWAYS so </s>
<s> For example, if you were to put before one of these Logicians the Pair of Premisses "No honest men cheat; No dishonest men are trustworthy" </s>
<s> and were to ask him what Conclusion followed, he would probably say "None at all </s>
<s> Your Premisses offend against TWO distinct Rules, and are as fallacious as they can well be" </s>
<s> Then suppose you were bold enough to say "The Conclusion is 'No men who cheat are trustworthy'," I fear your Logical friend would turn away hastily--perhaps angry, perhaps only scornful: in any case, the result would be unpleasant </s>
<s> I ADVISE YOU NOT TO TRY THE EXPERIMENT </s>
<s> "But why is this" </s>
<s> you will say </s>
<s> "Do you mean to tell us that all these Logicians are wrong" </s>
<s> Far from it, dear Reader </s>
<s> From THEIR point of view, they are perfectly right </s>
<s> But they do not include, in their system, anything like ALL the possible forms of Syllogisms </s>
<s> They have a sort of nervous dread of Attributes beginning with a negative particle </s>
<s> For example, such Propositions as "All not-x are y," "No x are not-y," are quite outside their system </s>
<s> And thus, having (from sheer nervousness) excluded a quantity of very useful forms, they have made rules which, though quite applicable to the few forms which they allow of, are no use at all when you consider all possible forms </s>
<s> Let us not quarrel with them, dear Reader </s>
<s> There is room enough in the world for both of us </s>

<s> Elementary </s>
<s> 1 </s>
<s> Whatever can be "attributed to", that is "said to belong to", a Thing, is called an 'Attribute' </s>
<s> For example, "baked", which can (frequently) be attributed to "Buns", and "beautiful", which can (seldom) be attributed to "Babies" </s>
<s> 2 </s>
<s> When they are the Names of two Things (for example, "these Pigs are fat Animals"), or of two Attributes (for example, "pink is light red") </s>
<s> 3 </s>
<s> When one is the Name of a Thing, and the other the Name of an Attribute (for example, "these Pigs are pink"), since a Thing cannot actually BE an Attribute </s>
<s> 4 </s>
<s> That the Substantive shall be supposed to be repeated at the end of the sentence (for example, "these Pigs are pink (Pigs)") </s>
<s> 5 </s>
<s> A 'Proposition' is a sentence stating that some, or none, or all, of the Things belonging to a certain class, called the 'Subject', are also Things belonging to a certain other class, called the 'Predicate' </s>
<s> For example, "some new Cakes are not nice", that is (written in full) "some new Cakes are not nice Cakes"; where the class "new Cakes" is the Subject, and the class "not-nice Cakes" is the Predicate </s>
<s> 6 </s>
<s> A Proposition, stating that SOME of the Things belonging to its Subject are so-and-so, is called 'Particular' </s>
<s> For example, "some new Cakes are nice", "some new Cakes are not nice" </s>
<s> A Proposition, stating that NONE of the Things belonging to its Subject, or that ALL of them, are so-and-so, is called 'Universal' </s>
<s> For example, "no new Cakes are nice", "all new Cakes are not nice" </s>
<s> 7 </s>
<s> The Things in each compartment possess TWO Attributes, whose symbols will be found written on two of the EDGES of that compartment </s>

<s> "One or more" </s>

<s> For example, "some new Cakes are nice and some are not-nice" </s>
